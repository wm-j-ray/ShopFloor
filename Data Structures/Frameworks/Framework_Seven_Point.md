# Seven-point Structure

> **Purpose:** Defines the 7-point structural sequence developed by Dan Wells for use in the Beat Registry and Conformance Report.
>
> **Instance naming:** When creating an instance, save as `[ID]_Name.md`, set `profile_version` and `date_instantiated`.

> 🔴 **TIER 1 — Core engine.** Complete before drafting begins.

built around reversals and escalating opposition. Especially useful for writers who
think in terms of plot engineering — where should the story be at each major point —
rather than character psychology.
external plot is minimal. The Seven-Point framework is plot-forward; internal change
is expressed through plot reversal, not explored through interiority.
Resolution first, then the Hook (its opposite), then works out how to get from one
to the other through five structural points. Every point is a reversal of state.
The protagonist moves from worst possible position to best, or best to worst.

## Common Failure Modes

1. The Hook and Resolution are not true opposites — the framework depends on maximum
contrast between the opening state and the ending state. If they are too similar,
the journey has no arc.
2. The Pinch Points are too mild — each Pinch must make the antagonist force feel
genuinely overwhelming. A Pinch that the protagonist brushes off fails its function.
3. The Midpoint is passive — the protagonist must shift from reactive to proactive at
the Midpoint. A protagonist who is still being acted upon in the second half has
not crossed the Midpoint structurally, regardless of where it appears on the page.

## Beat Definitions

| Beat ID | Beat Label | Required | Act | Target Position | Definition | Common Error |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| BT-001 | Hook | yes | 1 | 0-10% | The opening state — the protagonist's world before the story begins. Must be the direct opposite of the Resolution state. Establishes the baseline from which all change is measured. The reader must understand this world well enough to feel the distance traveled by the end. | The hook state is too close to the resolution state; the arc has insufficient range. |
| BT-002 | Plot Turn 1 | yes | 1 | 20-25% | The event that pulls the protagonist out of the Hook state and into the story's central conflict. The protagonist is introduced to the new situation — the problem, the quest, the threat — that will define the rest of the story. Marks the end of Act 1. | Too gentle; the protagonist's world is mildly disrupted rather than fundamentally changed. |
| BT-003 | Pinch 1 | yes | 2 | 37% | The antagonist force applies direct, overwhelming pressure. The protagonist is shown what they are truly up against — and it is worse than they understood. Forces the protagonist to commit more fully. | The antagonist force is underestimated by the writer as well as the protagonist; the threat does not feel credible. |
| BT-004 | Midpoint | yes | 2 | 50% | The protagonist shifts from reactive to proactive. No longer simply responding to the antagonist force — the protagonist takes deliberate action toward the resolution. A critical internal or external change of state drives this shift. | The protagonist is still reactive after the midpoint; they are being acted upon rather than acting. |
| BT-005 | Pinch 2 | yes | 2 | 62% | The antagonist force applies maximum pressure. Everything the protagonist has built or gained is threatened or lost. The situation appears completely hopeless. The lowest point of the story — functionally equivalent to All Is Lost and the Dark Night in other frameworks. | Weaker than Pinch 1; pressure should be escalating, not repeating. |
| BT-006 | Plot Turn 2 | yes | 3 | 75-80% | The protagonist discovers or acquires the final piece — knowledge, resource, internal shift — needed to resolve the conflict. The path to the Resolution becomes visible for the first time. Must arise from the protagonist's own effort or transformation, not from external rescue. | The discovery is handed to the protagonist by another character or by coincidence. |
| BT-007 | Resolution | yes | 3 | 90-100% | The opposite of the Hook — the protagonist's final state after the conflict is resolved. The dramatic question is answered. The core value has shifted completely from its opening position. The change is demonstrated through action, not declared through dialogue. | Resolution state is too similar to the Hook state; the journey has not produced genuine transformation. |

One entry per beat. Load directly into Beat Registry.

## Framework Notes

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| This framework maps cleanly to a three-act structure | Hook + Plot Turn 1 = Act 1, |

The Seven-Point Structure is designed to be applied backward. Before populating the
Beat Registry, define BT-007 (Resolution) first, then BT-001 (Hook) as its opposite,
then BT-004 (Midpoint) as the halfway transformation, then the remaining points.
All seven points are required — there are no optional beats in this framework.
This makes it the most structurally prescriptive framework in Write Track.
The two Pinch Points (BT-003 and BT-005) are unique to this framework. They function
as antagonist showcase moments — the reader (and protagonist) must feel the full
weight of the opposition at each Pinch.
Pinch 1 + Midpoint + Pinch 2 = Act 2, Plot Turn 2 + Resolution = Act 3.
Seven-Point works well in combination with Save the Cat — use Seven-Point for plot
engineering and Save the Cat for character and thematic layering.
