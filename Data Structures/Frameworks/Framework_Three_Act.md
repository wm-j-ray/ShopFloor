## Common Failure Modes

1. Act 2 sags — the middle is the longest act and the hardest to sustain. Without a
strong midpoint turn, Act 2 becomes a series of unconnected events rather than an
escalating pressure campaign.
2. The climax is not earned — if the protagonist wins by luck, coincidence, or external
rescue rather than by applying what they have learned or become, the resolution feels hollow.
3. Act 1 is too long — writers often over-establish. The inciting incident should arrive
no later than 20-25% into the story. Everything before it must be active, not passive setup.

## Beat Definitions

| Beat ID | Beat Label | Required | Act | Target Position | Definition | Common Error |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| BT-001 | Exposition | yes | 1 | 0-10% | Establishes the protagonist, their world, their flaw or lack, and the conditions of ordinary life before disruption. Introduces the central relationships and stakes. | Runs too long and becomes static; world-building crowds out dramatic momentum. |
| BT-002 | Inciting Incident | yes | 1 | 10-20% | The event that disrupts the ordinary world and launches the story's central conflict. Creates the dramatic question the story must answer. Must be specific, irreversible, and consequential. | Placed too late, or so minor that it fails to generate genuine forward momentum. |
| BT-003 | First Turning Point | yes | 1 | 20-25% | The end of Act 1 — a decisive event or decision that commits the protagonist to the central conflict and makes return to the ordinary world impossible. Raises the stakes significantly. | Happens passively; the protagonist is pushed into Act 2 rather than choosing to enter it. |
| BT-004 | Rising Action | yes | 2 | 25-75% | The bulk of the story — a series of escalating obstacles, reversals, and complications that press the protagonist toward a breaking point. Must build continuously, not plateau. | Loses momentum in the middle; scenes feel episodic rather than causally connected. |
| BT-005 | Midpoint | no | 2 | 50% | A significant shift at the story's center — a false victory, a false defeat, or a major revelation that raises the personal stakes and shifts the protagonist's strategy or understanding. | Treated as a minor scene rather than a structural pivot; the story feels identical on either side of it. |
| BT-006 | Second Turning Point | yes | 2 | 75% | The end of Act 2 — the lowest point. The protagonist's plan has failed, the antagonist force is at its strongest, and all seems lost. The protagonist must find a new resource — internal or external — to continue. | Arrives without sufficient buildup; the collapse feels unearned. |
| BT-007 | Climax | yes | 3 | 80-95% | The final confrontation between the protagonist and the central antagonist force. The dramatic question is answered. The protagonist must actively drive the resolution — it cannot be handed to them. | Won by coincidence, rescue, or luck rather than by the protagonist's demonstrated change or growth. |
| BT-008 | Resolution | no | 3 | 95-100% | The aftermath — the new equilibrium established after the climax. Shows the consequence of the protagonist's change or failure to change. Closes open narrative threads. | Omitted entirely; the story ends at the climax without showing the new world the conflict has created. |

## Framework Notes

Three-Act Structure defines proportions, not scenes. Act 1 is approximately 25% of the
total story, Act 2 approximately 50%, Act 3 approximately 25%.
The Midpoint (BT-005) is marked optional in this framework but is strongly recommended
for any story over 40,000 words. Without it, Act 2 loses structural integrity.
The Resolution (BT-008) is marked optional because some genres — thriller, horror —
end at or immediately after the climax. For literary and character-driven fiction it
is functionally required.
Three-Act is the lowest-resolution framework in Write Track. If a writer needs more
granular guidance, recommend Save the Cat or Seven-Point Structure instead.
