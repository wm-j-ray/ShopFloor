# Save The Cat

## Common Failure Modes

1. The theme is stated too on-the-nose — a character literally speaks the theme aloud
rather than embodying it through action.
2. The midpoint is too quiet — it must shift the protagonist from reactive to active,
or from active to reactive. A flat midpoint kills the second half.
3. The dark night of the soul is confused with the low point — it must be a moment of
genuine internal collapse, not just external setback.

## Beat Definitions

| Beat ID | Beat Label | Required | Act | Target Position | Definition | Common Error |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| BT-001 | Opening Image | yes | 1 | 0-1% | A single image or moment that captures the tone, world, and protagonist's current state. Must contrast with the Final Image to show change. | Chosen for visual interest rather than thematic contrast with the ending. |
| BT-002 | Theme Stated | yes | 1 | 5% | Someone — usually not the protagonist — states the theme or central question of the story, often without the protagonist understanding it yet. | Made too explicit; the protagonist agrees rather than resisting. |
| BT-003 | Setup | yes | 1 | 1-10% | Establishes the protagonist's ordinary world, their flaw, their want, and the people and stakes around them. Plants everything that will pay off later. | Runs too long; world-building crowds out character establishment. |
| BT-004 | Catalyst | yes | 1 | 10% | The inciting incident — an event that disrupts the ordinary world and makes the status quo impossible to maintain. Must happen to the protagonist, not be chosen by them. | Placed too late; reader loses patience before the story starts moving. |
| BT-005 | Debate | yes | 1 | 10-20% | The protagonist resists, hesitates, or argues against entering the new world or taking action. Raises the central dramatic question: will they or won't they? | Resolved too quickly; the protagonist agrees without genuine internal conflict. |
| BT-006 | Break Into Two | yes | 1 | 20% | The protagonist makes an active choice that commits them to the story's main action. The old world is left behind. This is the point of no return. | Happens passively — the protagonist is pushed into Act 2 rather than choosing it. |
| BT-007 | B Story | no | 2 | 20-25% | A secondary storyline — often a relationship — that carries the theme. The B Story is where the real internal change happens while the A Story handles external plot. | Treated as comic relief or subplot filler rather than the thematic spine. |
| BT-008 | Fun and Games | yes | 2 | 20-50% | The promise of the premise — what the reader came for. The protagonist explores the new world, pursues their want, and the central conflict generates its most entertaining material. | Allowed to go slack; the protagonist succeeds too easily or faces no meaningful opposition. |
| BT-009 | Midpoint | yes | 2 | 50% | A major shift — either a false victory that raises the stakes, or a false defeat that forces new strategy. Protagonist shifts from reactive to active or vice versa. Stakes become personal. | Treated as a minor scene transition rather than a structural hinge. |
| BT-010 | Bad Guys Close In | yes | 2 | 50-75% | External and internal opposition intensifies. The protagonist's plan begins to unravel. Team dynamics fracture. The antagonist force presses harder. | Runs at a single pitch; no variation in pressure causes reader fatigue. |
| BT-011 | All Is Lost | yes | 2 | 75% | The lowest point — the opposite of the Midpoint. An external defeat combined with an internal collapse. Often involves a death (literal or symbolic) near this beat. | Conflated with the Dark Night; must be event-driven, not just emotional. |
| BT-012 | Dark Night of Soul | yes | 2 | 75-80% | The protagonist sits with the loss. Internal reckoning. The false belief is examined, often for the first time with full honesty. The answer to the story's central question forms here. | Skipped or rushed; the protagonist recovers too quickly without genuine reckoning. |
| BT-013 | Break Into Three | yes | 3 | 80% | The protagonist finds the answer — synthesis of A Story and B Story — and commits to a final action. Must arise from internal change, not external rescue. | The solution comes from outside the protagonist rather than from their own transformation. |
| BT-014 | Finale | yes | 3 | 80-99% | The protagonist executes the plan, confronts the antagonist force, and the central conflict is resolved. Must demonstrate the new belief through action, not just declaration. | The climax is won by luck, coincidence, or a deus ex machina rather than earned character change. |
| BT-015 | Final Image | yes | 3 | 99-100% | A single image or moment that mirrors the Opening Image and proves the change — or the failure to change — is real. | Too similar to the opening; the contrast that proves transformation is absent. |

One entry per beat. Load directly into Beat Registry.

## Framework Notes

Save the Cat was written for screenplays but translates well to commercial fiction.
The 15 beats are percentage-based — they are positional, not scene-by-scene.
The B Story (BT-007) is optional in the Beat Registry but functionally essential for
character-driven work; mark it optional only if the A Story carries the theme alone.
The Fun and Games section (BT-008) is the longest single beat — it is not one scene
but a sequence. In the Beat Registry, link it to multiple Scene Containers.
