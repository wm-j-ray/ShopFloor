# Diarium Research Session

## Scene Development: Rob Berkley / Smitty Reunion Dinner

----

## 1. The Setup

**Bill's request:**

> I am stuck on a scene about a reunion, the first time two people, who have drifted apart over say, twenty years. They are going to get together, meeting for dinner in Bedminster, NJ. My protagonist, Rob Berkley, is very nervous about it because years ago, had an affair with Smitty's wife.

Identify any thoughts or fragments from my diary that will be a source of inspiration.

----

## 2. About the Diarium Data File

**File:** `Diarium_2003-08-16_2026-04-05.json`

**Date range:** August 16, 2003 — April 5, 2026 (22+ years of entries)

**Total entries:** 5,813

**Processing approach:** The JSON file was parsed programmatically in Python. Two search passes were run: the first cast a wide net across ~20 thematic keywords (reunion, affair, guilt, silence, shame, betrayal, regret, secret, awkward, etc.), returning 674 candidate entries. The second pass applied more targeted terms (reconnect, old wound, unspoken, weight of it, carry it, never said, meet again, two men, what do you say, etc.), returning 218 additional candidates. The most promising entries from both passes were pulled in full for close reading. Total entries reviewed in detail: approximately 25.

**Processing time:** Not clocked. Estimated under two minutes of active computation across all passes and full-text retrieval.

## 3. Results

What follows is organized by function — what each fragment does for the scene — rather than chronologically.

### THE WEIGHT ROB CARRIES (nervous interior)

**"Carrying a Brick" — July 5, 2016**
*(Source: The Rabbit Hole, film dialogue)*

The exchange between Diane Wiest and Nicole Kidman on grief and guilt:

> "No, I don't think it does. Not for me, it hasn't — has gone on for eleven years. But it changes though... the weight of it, I guess. At some point, it becomes bearable. It turns into something that you can crawl out from under and carry around like a brick in your pocket. And you... you even forget it, for a while. But then you reach in for whatever reason and — there it is."

This is Rob's guilt exactly. He has functioned for twenty years. The dinner is the reach.

----

**"On Regret" — January 31, 2024**
*(Compiled quotation collection)*

Two fragments from this entry cut hardest for this scene:

Sabaa Tahir's split:

> "There are two kinds of guilt: the kind that drowns you until you're useless, and the kind that fires your soul to purpose."

Which kind has Rob been carrying? Which kind walks into dinner?

Chuck Klosterman:

> "When you start thinking about what your life was like 10 years ago — and not in general terms, but in highly specific detail — it's disturbing to realize how certain elements of your being are completely dead... It's almost like those things didn't happen. Or maybe it seems like they just happened to someone else. To someone you don't really know."

Rob has used that distance as a coping mechanism. The reunion collapses it.

----

**"Mens Rea" — January 24, 2013**
*(Legal definition)*

> "As an element of criminal responsibility, a guilty mind; a guilty or wrongful purpose; a criminal intent. Guilty knowledge and wilfulness."

You clipped this for a reason. Rob knows what he did. There's no accidental reading of his affair. This sits underneath all of his social performance at dinner.

### THE REUNION MECHANICS (scene texture)

**"The Walk with Elizanne" / "Those Spoiled, Much Married Women" — December 28, 2023**
*(John Updike)*

The single most directly useful material in the diary. You pulled two Updike pieces on reunion. The Leila scene has Rob's scene in it almost beat for beat: the thumping heart on the long drive, the panic growing as he searches the streets, the glazed calm once he's actually in her presence:

> "His heart had been thumping throughout his long drive, to the point where he imagined an onset of fibrillations, and his panic had grown as he searched the blocks of Deerfield Beach... Now that he was here in Leila's presence, a kind of glazed calm, a sweat of suspension, came over him..."

And Updike's line — "It had been the nature of their old relationship for him simply to bring his body" — lands differently given Rob's history with Smitty's wife.

The Elizanne reunion piece adds the other register: a man at a reunion going through social motions with someone he has little to say to, performing pleasantness while his mind is elsewhere — *"he had little to say to her, and less than that to the tan and drawling husband... This man's indulgent witness was inhibiting."*

----

**"The Big Fade" — February 27, 2026**
*(Entry on the 1996 film Beautiful Girls)*

The film's central concept: the electric beginning of something eventually quiets into the domestic, the predictable, the porch-light glow. The entry's closing line:

> "It's about the realization that you can't live in a 10th-year high school reunion forever. Eventually, you have to go home to the person waiting for you, even if the 'shooting star' has settled into a steady, porch-light glow."

Useful less as direct scene material and more as the emotional reality underneath the dinner. Rob is face to face with a fade he caused. Whatever was lit between him and Smitty's wife — that's been dark for twenty years. What sits across the table from him now is the cost of it.

----

**"The Ballroom Dancers from Baltimore" — September 13, 2015**
*(Your own diary entry)*

Your observed scene of two couples going through social motions with almost nothing in common — small talk as noise, "I feigned attention." That's Rob at the table. Everything Smitty says becomes background while Rob watches and calculates. Your detail of catching only fragments of conversation ("I heard Pam. She said 'toilet paper.' That's all I got.") is a physical model for what selective attention under stress looks like at a dinner table.

### THE GHOST LOGIC (the past arriving)

**"Diamonds and Rust" — April 19, 2025**
*(Joan Baez)*

> "Here comes your ghost again..."
"We both know what memories can bring / They bring diamonds and rust."
"If you're offering me diamonds and rust / I've already paid."

And the vagueness line:

> "You who are so good with words / And at keeping things vague / 'Cause I need some of that vagueness now..."

That's what dinner in Bedminster is — two men keeping things vague. The question underneath all of it: how much do they say, and who goes first.

----

**"The Past" — July 3, 2023**
*(Bruce Springsteen)*

> "The past is never the past. It is always present. And you better reckon with it in your life and in your daily experience, or it will get you. It will get you really bad."

Rob has not reckoned. The dinner is the get.

----

**"St. Luke's" — July 3, 2023**
*(Your own diary entry)*

> "The place had a color and a damp and musty smell as if it were haunted by my parents long gone."

Your own description of returning to a place after twenty years. Bedminster needs that smell — some physical detail that returns Rob to a time he thought he'd sealed off. The restaurant, the parking lot, some mundane object. Something that makes the past tactile before Smitty even sits down.

----

**"If You Forget" — September 2, 2023**
*(Frederick Buechner, Whistling in the Dark)*

> "When you remember me, it means that you have carried something of who I am with you... It means that if we meet again, you will know me."

The question underneath the scene: does Smitty know? Being remembered is a form of existence. Rob fears being truly known. Smitty's recognition — of anything — is the disaster Rob is driving toward.

### CRAFT SCAFFOLDING (your own notes)

**"Why Journals Are A Good Thing" — August 13, 2020**
*(Your own diary entry on Wickersham/Story There)*

Your three-deaths framework maps cleanly onto this scene:

- *Physical death* — the affair ended something real between Rob and Smitty
- *Social/professional death* — the friendship, the twenty-year drift
- *Psychological death* — Rob's interior life since, carrying the brick

The scene diagnostic question: which death is this dinner about? All three are present. Which one surfaces?

----

**"Creating Compelling Characters — Everybody Lies" — May 1, 2013**
*(Writing craft note)*

> "Characters who seem strong can, in fact, be scared half to death. Characters who seem to be so caring can, in fact, be acting out of guilt, not genuine concern for others."

Rob's entire performance at dinner. Whatever warmth he brings — the handshake, the interest in Smitty's life, the listening — is contaminated. He knows it. The question is whether Smitty does.

----

### SUMMARY NOTE

The **Updike Leila piece** and the **Carrying a Brick** clip are the strongest raw material for the scene's interior mechanics. **Diamonds and Rust** gives the tonal register. **St. Luke's** gives the physical model for what it feels like to return to something after twenty years.

The Springsteen quote is the scene's thesis: Rob has not reckoned. Bedminster is the reckoning.
